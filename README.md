## Abstract

The increasing consumption of opioids represents a critical global public health problem due to their high addictive potential and severe physiological effects, particularly respiratory depression, which is the main cause of fatal overdose.

This project proposes a pharmacokinetic–pharmacodynamic (PK–PD) model that simulates the distribution and neural effects of opioids in the human body. The pharmacokinetic component models the intravenous administration of the drug, its transport through the bloodstream, metabolism in organs such as the liver, and its diffusion across the blood–brain barrier. The output of this stage is the opioid concentration reaching the central nervous system.

The pharmacodynamic component analyzes how this concentration affects different brain regions, including the ventral tegmental area, nucleus accumbens, prefrontal cortex, thalamus and brainstem. These subsystems describe the neuronal inhibition mechanisms associated with observable clinical effects such as analgesia, loss of consciousness and respiratory depression.

Additionally, a phenomenological layer provides a qualitative description of how opioids alter neuronal communication and neurochemical balance. This integrated approach enables the analysis and visualization of dose–response relationships and offers a computational framework for studying the physiological and neurological effects of opioid exposure.
